VAT and WHT on Nigerian Invoices: What Small Businesses Actually Need to Know

July 2026 · 5 min read

If you invoice other businesses in Nigeria, you've probably run into two acronyms that cause more confusion than they should: VAT and WHT. They're not interchangeable, they're not always both charged, and getting the order wrong on an invoice makes it look like you don't know what you're doing — even when the math is right.

VAT: you charge it, you remit it

Value Added Tax is currently 7.5% on most goods and services in Nigeria. When you invoice a client, VAT is added on top of your subtotal — the client pays it to you, and you're responsible for remitting it to FIRS. It's straightforward: subtotal plus 7.5% equals the VAT-inclusive amount before any other deductions.

WHT: the client deducts it, not you

Withholding Tax works the opposite way. For qualifying B2B services, the paying client deducts a percentage — commonly 5% or 10%, depending on the service type — before paying you, and remits that portion directly to FIRS on your behalf. You then get a WHT credit note you can set against your own tax liability later.

The order matters: VAT is added to the subtotal first, then WHT is deducted from that VAT-inclusive figure — not the other way around. Getting this backwards understates what you're actually owed.

A worked example

  • Subtotal for services rendered: ₦100,000
  • + VAT (7.5%): ₦7,500 → ₦107,500
  • − WHT (5%, on the ₦100,000 base): ₦5,000
  • = Amount the client actually pays you: ₦102,500

Note that WHT is typically calculated on the service subtotal, not the VAT-inclusive total — this trips up a lot of first-time invoicers.

When does WHT actually apply?

Generally: B2B services between companies, not typical retail sales to individual walk-in customers. If you're a freelancer or agency invoicing a registered company for consulting, rent, technical services, or similar, WHT usually applies. If you're selling products directly to consumers, it usually doesn't. When in doubt, ask your client's finance team — they're the ones deducting it, so they'll know their own obligation.
